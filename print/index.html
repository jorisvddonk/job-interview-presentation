The right mindset

You should know:

Interviewers are looking for someone... to fill in a certain role... that has the required skills... and displays no red flags.

If you made it past the resumé screening, they probably want you!

They might even be desperate to sign you!

When you work together with your interviewer, interviews will go smoothly.

...so you shouldn't:

  • Try to "convince" the interviewer(s) that you're the right person
  • Assume a different personality
  • Present knowledge or skills you don't have

..but please do:

  • Be honest
  • Focus on exposing the truth
  • Be critical of yourself and the company you're interviewing with
  • Be open about your weaknesses
  • See the process as a casual conversation to figure out if you could be of use
  • Take culture into account
  • Be glad that you didn't get the job if you think it was a bad fit

Interviews: keys to Success

Job interviews are like presentations...

Three keys to success

Be:

  • Confident
  • Comfortable
  • Enjoying it

After an interview, you'll be (implicitly) judged on two qualities:

Worthiness

  • Can you do the job?
  • Are you a good cultural fit?

Memorability

  • Fun
  • Unique
  • Engaging

Your skills make you worthy

Your personality and personal branding makes you memorable

The best thing about job interviews is that you can prepare for them

You will be asked:

  • To introduce yourself
  • What your key skills are
  • What your key weaknesses are
  • To explain why you left your previous job and/or country
  • To explain any gaps in your resumé
  • ...

It's good to come up with - and practice - your introduction.

You can pre-empt questions
Communicate who you are clearly

  • Come prepared; have questions ready! This shows interest.
  • Take notes during your conversation.
  • If there is a subsequent interview: figure out why you'd think you'll fit in well, and tailor your introduction accordingly.

War Stories

An ancient United States Military proverb:

Those who don’t have war stories, have not been deployed

An ancient Software Engineer proverb:

Those who don’t have war stories, have not been deployed

The act of writing software leads to broken software

By breaking software you learn things

The war stories you make along the way could be entertaining and informative

Sharing war stories in an interview can be:

  • Fun and informative
  • a way to get people to open up a bit
  • a way of signalling values and experience
  • an indication of a good cultural fit

They're also great fun at parties/meetups, and thus super easy to practice

Take-home assignments

These are projects that are time-constrained and tightly scoped, so that the interviewers can get an example of the quality of your work and have a reference point to base questions on in a subsequent interview.

Example

Hi Joris,

As part of our standard interview procedure, we'd like you to complete a short take-home assignment.

You should spend no more than 4 hours on this!

Your task is to create a simple online 2-player web-based Chess game engine. You must separate out the back-end from the front-end. In this chess engine, it must not be possible to make illegal moves.

Good luck!!

These are a great way to show 'em what you're made of!

A lot of people suck at these!

So if you "git gud"...

ez game ez life

Tip 1: As soon as you receive a take-home assignment:

  • Ask questions
  • State assumptions
  • State when you will be able to work on it / hand it in

Hi Person,

Would it be okay if I write this in TypeScript?

I'm going to assume that the reviewer of my project will be working on an environment in which they can run and build Docker containers.

I will be working on this during the weekend, and hope to send you the finished project before the end of Sunday.

Regards, Joris

Finally, do a retro & lookahead!

  • What went well?
  • What went wrong?
  • Did you learn anything new?
  • What would you do differently next time?
  • What would you add to the project if you had more time or wanted to expand on it?
    • Add this to your readme file!

More tips

  • Hand in your entire git repo! Mention that you have included the entire repo when handing it in.
  • Your git commits can influence and steer the conversation.
  • A good readme matters a lot. Spend time on it!
  • The easier a project is to get running, the better it is!
  • Write foolproof instructions; don't assume someone knows how npm works.
  • Mention if you got external help (e.g. from a friend/colleague) or spent more than the allotted amount of time on it.
  • Practice! Do small evening/weekend projects in your spare time!

If you do this well, your interviewers will be super impressed!
