## What you'll (hopefully) learn

Not skills to get <em>any</em> job/assignment, <br/> instead: tips to get jobs/assignments that <em>fit</em>.

<Notes>

By applying the tips from this presentation into your interviews, you'll _expose the truth_ 
about yourself and your skills, thereby making it more likely that you're going to find a _suitable_ job / assignment.
That does _not_ mean that you will _get the job_; it means that you will _get the right job_ that's suitable for _you_.

</Notes>

## You should know:

<span>Interviewers are looking for someone...
<Appear>
  <span> to fill in a certain <em>role</em>...</span>
  <span> that has the <em>required skills</em>...</span>
  <span> and displays <em>no red flags</em>.</span>
</Appear>
</span>

---

When you _work together_ with your interviewer to _dig up the truth_, interviews will go smoothly.

--- 

### ...so you shouldn't:

<ul>
<Appear>
  <li>Try to "convince" the interviewer(s) that you're the right person</li>
  <li>Assume a different personality</li>
  <li>Present knowledge or skills you don't have</li>
</Appear>
</ul>

---

### ..but please do:

<ul>
<Appear>
  <li>Be honest</li>
  <li>Focus on <em>exposing the truth</em></li>
  <li>Be critical of yourself and the company you're interviewing with</li>
  <li>Be open about your weaknesses</li>
  <li>See the process as a <em>casual conversation</em> to figure out if you could be of use</li>
  <li>Take <em>culture</em> into account</li>
  <li>Be glad that you didn't get the job if you think it was a bad fit</li>
</Appear>
</ul>

## For a smooth interview, it's good to come up with - and practice - your introduction.

---

### Example

<Notes>


My name is Joris van de Donk.
Rather than just believing that _infrastructure_ should be expressed as code, I believe that _anything_ should be expressed as code.
I consider myself to be an advocate of this practice, and hope to inspire and teach others to create fun, creative, engaging things with code.
Instead of creating presentations in Powerpoint or Google Slides, I prefer to program them myself using the latest web technologies.
This gives me flexibility, and allows me to create some cool things that otherwise wouldn't be easy to achieve.
I once programmed a presentation about making music using code, and made it so that I could use my electric guitar as a slide clicker. It went really well, despite the fact that I don't really know how to play the guitar, and had to ask the audience how to play certain chords!

This is information dense: it elaborates on how:
* I believe in expressing _anything_ as code, not just _infrastructure_.
* I am enthusiastic about making and holding presentations.
* I am opinionated and back opinions with motivations.
* I am creative and code in my spare time.
* Software engineering ain't just a _job_ to me.
* I like teaching and inspiring others to make cool things.


</Notes>

---

## More tips

* Come prepared; have questions ready! This shows interest.
* Take notes during your conversation.
* If there is a subsequent interview: figure out what you liked about the company, and mention reasons for why you'd think you'll fit in well.

# Take-home assignments

</Invert>

---

These are projects that are <em>time-constrained</em> and <em>tightly scoped</em>, so that the interviewers can <em>get an example of the quality of your work</em> and have a <em>reference point to base questions on</em> in a <em>subsequent interview</em>.

<Notes>

maybe elaborate on _why_ companies do these? e.g. they provide lots of info for the company whilst they need to dedicate fairly little time to it

</Notes>

---

## Example

<WrittenText>
  <p>Hi Joris,</p>
  <p>As part of our standard interview procedure, we'd like you to complete a short take-home assignment.</p>
  <p>You should spend no more than 4 hours on this!</p>
  <p>Your task is to create a simple online 2-player web-based Chess game engine. You must separate out the back-end from the front-end. In this chess engine, it must not be possible to make illegal moves.</p>
  <p>Good luck!!</p>
</WrittenText>

---

Tip 1: As soon as you receive a take-home assignment, you should:
* Ask questions
* State assumptions
* State when you will be able to work on it and hand it in

---

<WrittenText>
  <p>Hi Person,</p>
  <p>Would it be okay if I <b>write this in TypeScript?</b></p>
  <p>I'm going to <b>assume</b> that the reviewer of my project will be working on an environment <em>in which they can</em> <b>run and build Docker containers</b>.</p>
  <p>I will be working on this during the weekend, and hope to <b>send you the finished project before the end of Sunday</b>.</p>
  <p>Regards, Joris</p>
</WrittenText>

---

Tip 2: You should treat these projects like any other small project you work on !

## Finally, do a retro & lookahead!

<ul>
  <Appear>
  <li>What went well?</li>
  <li>What went wrong?</li>
  <li>What would you do differently next time?</li>
  <li>What would you add to the project if you <em>had more time</em> or <em>wanted to expand on the project</em>?</li>
  <b>Write all of this up in your readme file!</b>
</Appear>
</ul>

## More tips

<ul>
<Appear>
  <li>Hand in your entire git repo! Mention that you have included the entire repo when handing it in.</li>
  <li>Your git commits can influence and steer the conversation.</li>
  <li>A good readme matters <b>a lot</b>. Spend time on it!</li>
  <li>The easier a project is to get running, the better it is!</li>
  <li>Write foolproof instructions; don't assume someone knows how <inlineCode>npm</inlineCode> works.</li>
  <li>Mention if you got external help (e.g. from a friend/colleague) or spent more than the allotted amount of time on it.</li>
</Appear>
</ul>
